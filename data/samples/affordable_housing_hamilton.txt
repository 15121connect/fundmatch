Affordable Housing Retrofit Proposal for Hamilton

Executive Summary
This proposal outlines a comprehensive plan to retrofit existing affordable housing units in Hamilton, Ontario, to improve energy efficiency, reduce operational costs, and enhance living conditions for low-income residents. The project targets multi-unit residential buildings in underserved neighborhoods, focusing on insulation upgrades, energy-efficient windows, heating system replacements, and water conservation improvements.

Background and Rationale
Hamilton faces a growing shortage of affordable housing stock, with approximately 8,000 households on waitlists for subsidized units. Existing affordable housing buildings, many constructed in the 1970s-1990s, suffer from poor energy efficiency, high utility costs, and deferred maintenance. Data from local housing authorities indicates that average annual heating costs per unit are $1,200, compared to $800 for modern buildings—a $400 annual burden on residents already living below the poverty line.

Retrofitting these units presents a dual benefit: environmental impact through reduced carbon emissions and immediate cost relief for vulnerable populations. Studies from similar projects in Toronto and Ottawa document that retrofits achieve 30-40% reductions in energy consumption, translating to $100-150 savings per household annually.

Project Scope
- Target 500 affordable housing units across 12 buildings in Hamilton's east and north ends.
- Retrofit interventions: insulation, window replacement, HVAC system upgrades, LED lighting, smart thermostats.
- Timeline: 24 months from funding approval.
- Expected outcomes: 35% average energy reduction, $75,000 in cumulative resident savings over 5 years.

Partnerships and Governance
The project will operate under a collaborative governance model involving:
- City of Hamilton (municipal housing authority and planning support)
- Non-profit affordable housing organizations (project management and tenant engagement)
- Local contractors and tradespeople (labor and materials)
- McMaster University (energy auditing and baseline data collection)

Budget Estimate
Total project cost: $2.8 million
- Materials and labor: $2.2 million
- Energy audits and monitoring: $300,000
- Community outreach and tenant support: $200,000
- Project management and contingency: $100,000

Requested funding: $2.0 million (70% of project cost)
Co-funding: Municipal contributions ($500k), housing nonprofits ($300k)

Evaluation and Metrics
- Energy consumption reduction: Target 35% measured annually via utility billing data.
- Household savings: Track average utility bill reductions per unit.
- Tenant satisfaction: Annual survey measuring comfort improvements.
- Carbon offset: Calculate and report greenhouse gas emissions avoided.
- Replicability: Document lessons learned for scaling to other municipalities.

Conclusion
This retrofit initiative combines environmental stewardship with social equity, demonstrating that energy efficiency improvements can directly improve quality of life for low-income residents while advancing municipal climate commitments. The detailed planning, strong partnerships, and clear metrics position this as a model for affordable housing decarbonization across Ontario.
